I'd push toward the higher end for the chaser himself — "16-bit maybe 32" doesn't have to be an either/or, and there's actually a good reason to use both:

Base Neckbeard — cruder, lower tier. Think SNES-era (Streets of Rage, Secret of Mana): ~32-48px sprite, a tight 12-16 color palette, 4-6 frame walk cycle. This actually suits his character — he's a low-effort loser, so his sprite can look a little scrappy on purpose.

Revenant Neckbeard + the Undead Waifu — bump up to the denser, late-90s arcade tier: Neo Geo/Metal Slug/King of Fighters territory. ~64-96px sprites, 20-32 colors, way more shading and animation frames. That era is famous for absurdly over-animated, exaggerated sprite work, which fits the "escalation" tonally too.

The reason to split it rather than pick one: fidelity jump doubles as a difficulty tell. When he resurrects and the art itself gets denser and better-shaded, that reads as "this got more dangerous" without any UI or text — same trick games use for boss/elite-enemy upgrades. It also gives you a cheaper v1 — you only need to nail the scrappier tier to ship the first form, and can invest the higher-fidelity work into the form players see less often.

Also worth it regardless of which tier(s) you land on: the character concept (sweat, jiggle, disgusted grin, a legible meme shirt graphic) needs enough pixels to read — a tiny 16-bit-only sprite risks turning into an unreadable blob. If you only want one tier throughout, I'd lean toward the denser one for that reason, not the cruder one.

A few implementation notes since this is rendering over arbitrary webpages, not a fixed console framebuffer:

Author at native low resolution, scale up by integer multiples only (2x/3x/4x) with image-rendering: pixelated in CSS — non-integer scaling blurs pixel edges and kills the look, especially on high-DPI monitors.
Decouple movement from animation: his on-screen position can interpolate smoothly at 60fps (needed for the steering/chase feel), while the sprite itself frame-steps at a chunky 8-12fps. That's how the original games got fluid motion out of only a handful of hand-drawn frames.
Pick a fixed, small palette per character up front (in something like Aseprite) — keeps every animation frame consistent instead of colors drifting frame to frame.
The visual clash of a pixel-art sprite crawling across a modern flat/vector webpage is a feature, not a bug — it should look like it doesn't belong there, because it doesn't.

Checked, since this deserved an actual answer rather than a guess — and it turns out yes, there's a surprisingly on-point precedent here.

In 2012, the real man in the video (who's stayed publicly anonymous by choice) sued the filmmaker who'd been monetizing it — and won. The lawsuit specifically covered "3D characters, comics and more" that the filmmaker had created off the video, not just the raw footage. The court ordered him to stop publishing the man's image, pay about €23,000 total in damages and costs, and — this is the part that answers your question directly — ruled that even pixelating his face wasn't enough, because he was still recognizable. So drawn/rendered/abstracted versions were explicitly part of what got ruled against here, not just the literal clip.

That makes this one meaningfully different from most of the rest of the roster. Pepe, Doge, Trollface — those are drawn characters. This is a real, identifiable, non-consenting person, and the one time it went to court, "we made our own version of it" wasn't a defense that held up.

Practically: a personal, unpublished browser toy referencing him is still a very different risk profile than a filmmaker actively monetizing derivative content, which is what actually got sued. But if you want the joke without touching that specific risk at all, the easy fix is the same move as Leeroy — build an original "intimidating pointing viking" archetype that's clearly its own character rather than a recognizable rendering of that specific guy.
